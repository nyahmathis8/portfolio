Final Project

A look into the changing demographics of New York City...

NEW YORK — Over the last two decades, the story of New York City has been a story about who got to stay. Across dozens of neighborhoods, rents have climbed and the makeup and demographics of entire communities have changed completely.

An example of this happening is Dumbo, a Brooklyn neighborhood tucked between the Manhattan and Brooklyn Bridges. The small neighborhood went from fewer than 110 residents in 2000 to more than 1,100 by 2016. The median rent there nearly quadrupled over that same period, rising from around $700 a month to nearly $2,900. Dumbo quickly became one of the most expensive zip codes in Brooklyn, and the transformation happened so fast that many of the longtime residents barely had time to notice before it was complete. Bushwick is the same, with the non-Hispanic white population growing by ten times or more in some parts of the neighborhood between 2000 and 2016 while rent doubled and the share of college-educated residents climbed. In Harlem and Crown Heights, neighborhoods that had been backbones of Black and Latino life in New York for generations, hundreds of new white residents moved in over those years while family incomes rose and rents rose with them.

As wealthier residents move into these neighborhoods, many of the people who have lived there for years and decades find themselves unable to keep up with rising costs. Stuart Rosenthal, a real estate economist at Syracuse University who has studied gentrification and neighborhood change for decades, referenced research from UC Berkeley that directly addresses this problem. According to the research, 2016 more than one third of low-income households across the New York region were living in neighborhoods already under gentrification pressure, and that over 12 percent of neighborhoods citywide were actively gentrifying or had already reached an advanced stage of change (urbandisplacement.org).

What made the situation harder was that these rising costs are not just in neighborhoods that have recently attracted new investment. In places like Mott Haven and Melrose in the South Bronx, rents rose significantly even as incomes remained the same, leaving residents with fewer options and none of the improved services or amenities that sometimes accompany change in other neighborhoods.

By far, Brooklyn has had the most visible transformation. Five of its neighborhoods recorded the largest increases in white residents paired with simultaneous decreases in Black or Latino residents between 2000 and 2016. In Bedford-Stuyvesant, Williamsburg, Clinton Hill, Park Slope, and Crown Heights North, each neighborhood gained thousands of new white residents while the minority communities that had built and sustained those places for decades shrank. In Fort Greene and Clinton Hill specifically, the non-Hispanic white population more than doubled, rents rose by 150 percent or more, and families of color steadily moved away, first to other parts of Brooklyn, then to Queens, and in many cases out of the city altogether.

Between 2020 and 2024, citywide rents rose roughly 17 percent above pre-pandemic levels, and the city's own Displacement Risk Index now flags dozens of neighborhoods as being at serious risk. These places are where low incomes, deteriorating housing, and rising market pressure are all combined.

New York has always been a city that changes, and neighborhoods have shifted throughout its history as waves of newcomers arrived and communities evolved. But the scale and speed of what took place between 2000 and 2016 was something different, driven by the economics of a housing market that consistently rewarded those with wealth and punished those without it. The people who paid the highest price were Black and Latino residents and low-income renters, which are communities that had endured decades of disinvestment only to find that when investment finally arrived, it came at a cost they could not afford.
